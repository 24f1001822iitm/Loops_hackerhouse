I. The Hold

Your call is important to us. Forty-seven minutes important.

You dial the number on the back of the card. A voice, too cheerful for the occasion, asks you to say or enter your policy number. You do. It asks again. A menu branches, then branches again — press 2 for auto, 3 for property, 4 for something that might be your situation but you're not sure. The music arrives. It is always the same music. You are transferred once, and then again, and somewhere in the second transfer the line explains that due to unusually high call volume, wait times are longer than usual. They have been longer than usual for years.

You pay to be covered. You end up on hold.

The numbers are blunt. Average hold time at a Tier-1 U.S. insurer during incident surges: 38 minutes. Share of callers who hang up before reaching a human: 41%. Cost per routed call to the insurer, once you include the IVR, the transfer, the agent's time, the after-call work: $7.50. A single hailstorm across three counties can push a call center past capacity for a week.

None of this is because the call center is evil. The people inside it are, in the main, trying. They are outnumbered. Claims volume does not arrive evenly — it arrives in cliffs, triggered by weather, by accidents, by fraud rings. A staffing model that answers the average day chokes on the bad one. There aren't enough humans to staff a five-minute answer. There might be enough Alex.

II. Alex's Tools

Six things she can do on a call.

Alex's intelligence is the combination of ElevenLabs voice synthesis and six small, specific tools — each a webhook that runs against the insurer's own backend and returns in under 500 milliseconds. Miss that window and she can't sound human.

lookup_claim

Find the claim.

Looks up an existing claim by number; returns status, adjuster, and documents on file.

typically 312ms claim_id: CLM-2026-000456

check_policy

Check the coverage.

Pulls a policy by number; returns what it covers and its deductible, line by line.

typically 188ms policy_number: POL-2024-001234

check_documents

See what's missing.

Compares the required documents against what's already been received for a given claim.

typically 204ms claim_id: CLM-2026-000456

file_claim

File a new one.

Creates a new claim with type, date, and description; returns a fresh claim number on the line.

typically 412ms claim_type: collision

escalate_to_human

Hand off when stuck.

Writes an escalation with reason and priority; routes the caller to a supervisor queue.

typically 156ms priority: urgent

schedule_callback

Call back later.

Parses a natural-language time ("tomorrow afternoon") and books a callback on the calendar.

typically 244ms preferred_time: "tomorrow 2pm"

All six hit the same Fastify backend, on Railway, backed by Supabase. Add your own, and Alex learns them on deploy.

IV. If you have a policy

You pay to be covered. Not to be on hold.

Something breaks at the wrong hour — a cracked windshield on the way to work, a ceiling that starts leaking right as the guests arrive, a fender that crumples in a parking lot at 11 p.m. You reach for the phone and remember: the number on the back of the card is only staffed during business hours, and what answers first is a menu of seven options, none of which is yours.

“It’s 2 a.m. Your basement is flooding. Alex picks up on the second ring.”

Alex changes the shape of that call. She’s available at any hour, already knows your policy by the time you’ve said your name, and can file the claim by voice while you’re still standing in the water. She texts you a reference number before you hang up, and she hands the line to a human the moment you ask — or the moment the situation actually needs one.

Alex only sees what your insurer lets her see. Every tool call hits your insurer’s own backend. No conversation data leaves their system unless you ask it to.

V. If You Run a Claims Line

An average claims call costs $7.50 to handle. Alex costs fourteen cents.

Roughly eighty percent of the calls that land on a claims line are routine: status checks, policy questions, new filings, document nudges. They clog the queue, tire out your reps, and push the median hold time past anything a regulator likes to see. They are also, almost without exception, the calls your reps least want to take.

Alex handles that first bucket on your existing phone line. The other twenty percent — the ones with a grieving spouse, a disputed total loss, a suspected fraud signal — she hands off to a human, with full context and a reference number already written into your claim system.

VII. Common Questions

What people ask, before they call.

Will my callers know they're talking to an AI?

Alex identifies herself as an assistant by name when the call opens. If a caller asks whether they're on with a human, she says no and offers to transfer. Most callers don't ask — they ask about their claim.

What happens when Alex doesn't know the answer?

She runs escalate_to_human, creates an escalation record with the reason and a priority, and hands off with a reference number. The human adjuster gets the transcript and the tools she fired before they pick up.

Does it support multiple languages?

English (US) and Spanish (US) at launch, with Mandarin in closed beta. Voice, LLM, and prompts are swapped per language — not machine-translated on the fly.

How does it handle data privacy?

Every tool call hits your backend — not ours. Alex never stores policyholder PII. Transcripts are retained only in your Supabase instance, subject to your row-level security policies.

What if a caller just wants a human?

Alex offers the transfer on the first ask, without negotiation. The goal is 78–86% deflection, not 100%. The remaining 20% is exactly what human agents should be spending their time on.

How fast can we deploy on our line?

One day for a pilot. Point a Twilio number at the ElevenLabs agent, paste six webhook URLs, upload your knowledge base, and dial. Production hardening (SLAs, monitoring) is a separate conversation.
