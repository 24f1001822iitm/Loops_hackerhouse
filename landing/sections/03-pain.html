I. The Hold

Your call is important to us. Forty-seven minutes important.

You dial the number on the back of the card. A voice, too cheerful for the occasion, asks you to say or enter your policy number. You do. It asks again. A menu branches, then branches again — press 2 for auto, 3 for property, 4 for something that might be your situation but you're not sure. The music arrives. It is always the same music. You are transferred once, and then again, and somewhere in the second transfer the line explains that due to unusually high call volume, wait times are longer than usual. They have been longer than usual for years.

You pay to be covered. You end up on hold.

The numbers are blunt. Average hold time at a Tier-1 U.S. insurer during incident surges: 38 minutes. Share of callers who hang up before reaching a human: 41%. Cost per routed call to the insurer, once you include the IVR, the transfer, the agent's time, the after-call work: $7.50. A single hailstorm across three counties can push a call center past capacity for a week.

None of this is because the call center is evil. The people inside it are, in the main, trying. They are outnumbered. Claims volume does not arrive evenly — it arrives in cliffs, triggered by weather, by accidents, by fraud rings. A staffing model that answers the average day chokes on the bad one. There aren't enough humans to staff a five-minute answer. There might be enough Alex.
